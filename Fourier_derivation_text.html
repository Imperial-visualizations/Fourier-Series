Introduction to Fourier series

Consider a function $f(x)$ which is periodic over the domain $-L < x < L $ where $L$ is positive. $f(x)$ can be represented as a sum of $\sin$ and $\cos$ with varying "frequencies". These trigonometric functions form orthogonal basis.

The Fourier series of $f(x)$ is

$ f(x) = \displaystyle\frac{a_0}{2}$ $+ \displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \Big[ a_n\cos\Big(\frac{\pi nx}{L}\Big)$ $+ \displaystyle b_n\sin\Big(\frac{\pi nx}{L}\Big) \Big]$ $ \qquad \left[ 1 \right]$

where

$$ a_n = \displaystyle\frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^{L} f(x) \cos\Big(\frac{\pi nx}{L}\Big) dx, $$ $$ b_n = \displaystyle\frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^{L} f(x) \sin\Big(\frac{\pi nx}{L}\Big) dx, $$ $$ a_0 = \displaystyle\frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^{L} f(x) dx, $$

and

$$ n=0,1,2...$$

Note: $a_0$$/2$ represents the average value of $f(x)$.

The Fourier series representation of the function $f(x)$ in exponential (complex) form is

$$ f(x) = \displaystyle\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n e^{{in \pi x}/L} \qquad \left[ 2 \right]$$

where

$$ c_n = \displaystyle\frac{1}{2L}\int_{-L}^{L} f(x) e^{{-in \pi x}/L} dx, $$

Note: The constants in front of the integral(s) of the coefficient for the trigonometric and exponential fourier series are different. Also note the limit of the summations.

Derivation of the Fourier Series

We begin by assuming that $f(x)$ can be represented as a sum of exponential terms.

$$ f(x) = \displaystyle\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n e^{{in \pi x}/L}$$

Multiplying by $e^{{im \pi x}/L}$, where $m=0,1,2...$, and integrating both sides gives the following:

$\displaystyle\int_{-L}^{L} f(x) e^{{im \pi x}/L} dx$ $=\displaystyle\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n \int_{-L}^{L} e^{{i(n-m) \pi x}/L} dx $

Performing the integral on the R.H.S gives

$ \displaystyle\int_{-L}^L e^{(i\pi x/L)(n-m)}dx$ $\displaystyle =\left\{ \begin{array}{ll} 2L \qquad \text{(if n=m)} \\ 0 \qquad \quad \text{(if nâ‰ m)} \end{array} \right \} $ $\displaystyle= 2L \cdot \delta_{nm}$

This is due to orthonormality of complex exponentials.

As the only non-zero value of the sum is when $n=m$

$\displaystyle\int_{-L}^L f(x) e^{-im\pi x/L}dx$ $\displaystyle= 2L \sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}c_n\delta_{mn}$ $\displaystyle=2L\cdot c_m $

Hence

$\displaystyle c_m=\frac{1}{2L}\int_{-L}^{+L}f(x) e^{-im\pi x/L}dx $

Note: As $m$ is an arbitrary number, it can be assigned as $n$ for simplicity.

Given the previously derived Eqn., we can derive the trigonometric form of the Fourier series. By reorganising the sum, we get

$\displaystyle f(x)= \underbrace{c_0}_\text{n=0}$ $\displaystyle + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \big( \underbrace{c_n e^{in\pi x/L}}_\text{Positive n term}$ $\displaystyle + \underbrace{c_{-n} e^{-in\pi x/L}}_\text{Negative n terms} \big)$

We can regroup the terms in the summation in the following way:

$ \displaystyle = \frac{1}{2} \Big[ 2c_n e^{in\pi x/L}+ 2c_{-n} e^{-in\pi x/L}$ $ \displaystyle + \underbrace{c_{-n} e^{in\pi x/L} - c_{-n} e^{in\pi x/L}}_\text{ =0}$ $\displaystyle + \underbrace{c_{n} e^{-in\pi x/L} -c_{n} e^{-in\pi x/L}}_\text{ =0} \Big] $ $ \displaystyle = \frac{1}{2} \Big[ c_n e^{in\pi x/L} +c_{-n} e^{in\pi x/L}$ $ \displaystyle + c_n e^{-in\pi x/L} + c_{-n} e^{-in\pi x/L}$ $ \displaystyle + c_n e^{in\pi x/L} - c_{-n} e^{in\pi x/L}$ $ \displaystyle - c_n e^{-in\pi x/L} +c_{-n} e^{-in\pi x/L} $
$\displaystyle f(x)= c_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}$ $\displaystyle \Big( \frac{c_n + c_{-n}}{2} \big(e^{in\pi x/L}+e^{-in\pi x/L} \big)$ $ \displaystyle \frac{c_n - c_{-n}}{2} \big(e^{in\pi x/L}-e^{-in\pi x/L} \big) \Big) $

Now take $ a_n = c_n + c_{-n}$ and $ b_n = i(c_n - c_{-n})$ and notice the exponentials can we written as $\sin$ and $\cos$.

Note: $a_0 = 2c_0$ and $b_0=0$.

Hence

$ f(x) = \displaystyle\frac{a_0}{2}$ $+ \displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \Big[ a_n\cos\Big(\frac{\pi nx}{L}\Big)$ $+ \displaystyle b_n\sin\Big(\frac{\pi nx}{L}\Big) \Big]$

$a_n$ can be derived by using the expression from $c_n$

$\displaystyle a_n = c_n + c_{-n}$ $\displaystyle =\frac{1}{2L}\int_{-L}^{+L}f(x) e^{-in\pi x/L}dx$ $\displaystyle +\frac{1}{2L}\int_{-L}^{+L}f(x) e^{in\pi x/L}dx$

Factorise and spot the exponential form of $\cos$.

$\displaystyle =\frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^{+L}f(x)$ $\displaystyle \Big[\frac{e^{in\pi x/L} +e^{-in\pi x/L}}{2}\Big]dx$ $\displaystyle= \displaystyle\frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^{L} f(x) \cos\Big(\frac{\pi nx}{L}\Big) dx$

Similar method can be used to derived Eqn. for $b_n$.

$ b_n = \displaystyle\frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^{L} f(x) \sin\Big(\frac{\pi nx}{L}\Big) dx $

Triangular Fourier Series Derivation

The triangular function is defined by the following:

$\displaystyle f(x)=\left\{ \begin{array}{ll} \frac{-2A}{L} (x+L) \quad (-L < x < -L/2) \\ \frac{2A}{L} x \qquad \qquad (-L/2< x < L/2) \\ \frac{-2A}{L} (x-L) \quad (L/2< x < L) \end{array} \right. $

First notice that $f(x)$ is odd about $x=0$, hence $a_n=0$ for all $n$. Also,

$\displaystyle b_n=\frac{2}{L}\int_0^L f(x)\cdot \sin \left(\frac{n \pi x}{L}\right)dx $

since the $b_n$ integrand is even about $x=0$.

Notice that for even $n$, $b_n$ is odd about $x=L/2$. Hence

$ \displaystyle b_n=\frac{2}{L}\int_0^{L} f(x) \sin \left(\frac{n\pi x}{L} \right) dx = 0 \quad \text{for even n} $

The integral from $0$ to $L/2$ is equal in magnitude but opposite in sign to the integral from $L/2$ to $L$, for even $n$.

For odd $n$, the function is even around $L/2$, so we only have to integrate from $0$ to $L/2$ then multiply by $2$.This has made our job much easier.

$\displaystyle b_n=2\frac{2}{L}\int_0^{L/2} f(x) \sin \left(\frac{n\pi x}{L} \right) dx \quad \text{for odd n} $

Play around with the visualisation to the right to convince yourself this is true.

$ \displaystyle \text{For 0 < x < L/2:} \quad f(x)=\frac{2Ax}{L} $

Remembering that for even $n$, $b_n=0$, therefore only consider odd $n$.

$\displaystyle b_n = \frac{4}{L} \frac{2A}{L}\int_0^{L/2} x \sin \left(\frac{n\pi x}{L} \right) dx $
$\displaystyle u=x \qquad dv= \sin \left(\frac{n\pi x}{L} \right)dx $

$\displaystyle du=dx \qquad v=\frac{-L}{n\pi} \cos \left(\frac{n\pi x}{L} \right) $ $\displaystyle b_n=\frac{8A}{L^2}\cdot \left[\frac{-xL}{n\pi}\cdot \cos \left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)|_0^{L/2}-\int_0^{L/2}\frac{-L}{n\pi}\cdot \cos \left(\frac{n\pi x}{L} \right)dx \right] $

For odd n,

$\displaystyle \cos \left(\frac{n\pi}{2} \right)=0 $

Therefore,

$\displaystyle b_n=\frac{8A}{L^2}\cdot \left[0+\int_0^{L/2}\frac{L}{n\pi}\cdot \cos \left(\frac{n\pi x}{L} \right)dx \right]$
$\displaystyle b_n=\frac{8A}{L^2} \frac{L^2}{(n\pi)^2} \cdot \sin \left(\frac{n\pi x}{L} \right) \biggr |_0^{L/2} $

Note for odd $n$ :

$ \displaystyle \sin \left(\frac{n\pi}{2} \right)=(-1)^{\frac{n-1}{2}} $

Therefore,

$ \displaystyle b_n=\frac{8A}{n^2\pi^2}(-1)^{\frac{n-1}{2}} $

Finally,

$\displaystyle f(x)=\sum_{n=1,3,5...}^\infty \frac{8A}{n^2\pi^2}(-1)^{\frac{n-1}{2}} \cdot \sin \left(\frac{n\pi x}{L} \right) $

And the using that an odd number is given by $2n +1$ for any $n$,

$ \displaystyle f(x)=\sum_{n=1,2,3...}^\infty\frac{8A}{(2n-1)^2\pi^2}(-1)^n \cdot \sin \left(\frac{(2n-1)\pi x}{L} \right) $
